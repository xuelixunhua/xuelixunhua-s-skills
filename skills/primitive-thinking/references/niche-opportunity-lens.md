# Niche Opportunity Lens Reference

## Core Idea

High-growth products do not always start from hot markets or glamorous technology. Many start from a narrow, annoying, repeated workflow inside a specific industry, then productize the human coordination, judgment, and paperwork that used to hold that workflow together.

The question is not "which market is huge?" or "which model is newest?" The better question is:

What workflow is painful, frequent, role-based, budget-connected, and newly productizable because AI lowers the cost of handling messy information and repeated operations?

## Article-Derived Anchors

- Fast-growing SaaS companies may look like "small weird goods": clinic operations, fashion sample tracking, hospital PDF/fax processing, wealth advisor operations, relationship intelligence.
- Their shared pattern is not advanced technology. It is taking a frustrating, repeated, coordination-heavy vertical workflow and making it into product.
- Good entry points are narrow, but the workflow behind them is deep.
- Customers buy ROI: saving labor, reducing errors, increasing throughput, reducing compliance risk, or making one person able to handle more work.
- Many opportunities are hidden in overlooked small markets because the old process relied on manual coordination.
- AI is useful when it takes over concrete role actions, not when it merely adds a generic chat box.
- The core logic remains: find the right scene first, then choose technology.

## Opportunity Tests

Use these tests to judge a direction:

- **Narrow Entry, Deep Workflow**: Can you enter through one painful task but expand into the surrounding daily workflow?
- **Role Action Test**: Which human role action does this replace or augment: assistant, coordinator, analyst, front desk, operator, reviewer, compliance checker?
- **ROI Test**: Can the buyer calculate saved time, saved headcount, fewer errors, faster revenue, or lower risk?
- **Coordination Test**: Does the workflow involve three or more roles, handoffs, approvals, reminders, or status tracking?
- **Messy Data Test**: Are important inputs trapped in PDFs, emails, chat logs, spreadsheets, scans, screenshots, faxes, or human memory?
- **Error Cost Test**: Are mistakes expensive because of money, health, law, compliance, reputation, or customer loss?
- **Industry Knowledge Test**: Does success require field names, workflows, regulations, exceptions, habits, and integrations that outsiders do not know?
- **Expansion Test**: After solving the entry task, can the product naturally expand into adjacent workflow steps?

## AI-Era Product Direction Taxonomy

### 1. Industry AI Operator

Do not build a general copilot. Build an operator for a concrete role action.

Examples:

- Medical: summarize records, prepare patient communication, extract insurance documents.
- Wealth advisory: segment clients, prepare compliance review, produce advisor operations tasks.
- Education: grade assignments, adjust learning paths, prepare parent/student feedback.
- Marketing: produce content, execute campaigns, close the attribution loop.

Good sign: the product can complete a job that previously required an assistant, coordinator, analyst, reviewer, or operations person.

### 2. Unstructured Data Productization

Turn messy inputs into workflow-ready data.

Look for industries where critical information lives in:

- PDF
- email
- Word or Excel files
- chat screenshots
- scanned documents
- faxes
- call notes
- legacy system exports

The primitive candidate is often the normalized entity, extracted event, searchable index, or rule-checked record.

### 3. Front Office Plus Back Office Integration

Connect customer-facing acquisition/communication with internal operations.

Single-point tools often hit ceilings. Products become harder to replace when they connect:

- booking + reminder + record
- intake + verification + execution
- lead + relationship + referral + follow-up
- customer message + internal task + outcome tracking

### 4. Software Plus Service

Start by delivering outcomes with service, then gradually productize the repeated service actions.

This is useful when:

- the customer wants results more than software
- workflow details are hard to learn without doing the work
- data and edge cases emerge during delivery
- trust must be earned before automation can be accepted

Risk: staying a service business forever. The exit path is to continuously convert repeated service work into product primitives.

### 5. Lightweight Industry Operating System

Small organizations often need ERP-like coordination but cannot tolerate heavy ERP.

Examples:

- small clinics
- small law firms
- training institutions
- boutique agencies
- professional service teams
- local logistics or repair businesses

Good product shape: light enough to adopt, specific enough to match the industry's real workflow, and deep enough to become the daily operating system.

## Before-Build Planning

When evaluating a new direction:

1. Name the narrow workflow, not just the industry.
2. Identify the buyer, daily operator, and affected roles.
3. Map the current manual process: inputs, handoffs, decisions, outputs, errors.
4. Quantify ROI in the buyer's language: saved hours, saved headcount, reduced mistakes, faster cash collection, lower risk, higher capacity.
5. Identify the AI leverage point: extraction, classification, generation, decision support, routing, monitoring, or execution.
6. Identify the primitive candidate hidden inside: entity, action, rule, data feed, event stream, or agent tool.
7. Decide whether to start as pure software, software-plus-service, or a narrow automation wedge.

## Post-Build Reflection

When reviewing an existing product:

1. Ask whether it is attached to a painful vertical workflow or only a generic productivity desire.
2. Check whether the customer can explain ROI without your help.
3. Find where humans still do repeated coordination around the product.
4. Look for unstructured data that remains outside the system.
5. Identify whether the product is too horizontal and should narrow into a role, industry, or workflow.
6. Decide whether service delivery would reveal missing workflow knowledge.
7. Convert repeated role actions into primitive candidates.

## Red Flags

- The product is "AI for everyone" or "copilot for everything."
- The market sounds large, but no single workflow is painful enough.
- The user likes the tool, but the buyer cannot justify budget.
- The product saves clicks but not labor, errors, risk, or revenue leakage.
- There is no messy data, handoff, compliance issue, or repeated role action.
- The team is using AI as a feature label rather than as a way to take over a concrete operation.

## Output Pattern

For each opportunity candidate, capture:

- Niche workflow: the specific process, not the broad market.
- Roles: buyer, operator, affected roles.
- Current pain: manual steps, coordination, messy data, error cost.
- ROI: why someone pays now.
- AI leverage: what AI makes cheaper or newly possible.
- Primitive candidate: what reusable object/action/data/rule might emerge.
- Wedge: first narrow product or software-plus-service offer.
- Expansion: adjacent workflow steps.
- Risks: adoption, trust, workflow complexity, market size, service trap.
